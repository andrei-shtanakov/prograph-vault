## 1. Chrome 150 lands in stable and finally kills off uBlock Origin

**What happened:** On June 30, Google rolled Chrome 150 out to the stable channel and, along with it, removed the `ExtensionManifestV2Disabled` flag — the last mechanism that let users and enterprise admins keep Manifest V2 extensions enabled. This means the effective end of uBlock Origin (40+ million users) and other full-featured blockers built on dynamic filtering.

**Technical details:** Manifest V3 caps the number of static filtering rules and removes the dynamic `webRequest` blocking that the "classic" uBlock relies on. What remains is uBlock Origin Lite on declarativeNetRequest — without cosmetic filtering and with trimmed-down lists. Chrome 150 also shipped an IndexedDB backend rewritten on SQLite (Notion, Figma, offline Google Docs) and WebUI Refresh 2026.

**My take:** The finale dragged on for years, and now it has arrived — and let's be clear-eyed about it: this isn't about "extension security," it's about Google's advertising business model baked into the browser engine. Technically, declarativeNetRequest is a reasonable API from a performance and privacy standpoint, but the rule ceiling was chosen precisely so as to knock out of the game the single most effective tool against Google's own ads. For developers, the takeaway is simple: the Chromium monoculture is dangerous, and the only real counterweight right now is Firefox, which preserved webRequest compatibility. If I were among those for whom content filtering is critical, I'd diversify my browser fleet now rather than wait for "Lite" to catch up to the original — it won't, by design.

## 2. South Korea bets $576 billion on chips: Samsung and SK Hynix to build two new fabs each

**What happened:** On June 29, the South Korean government unveiled a national semiconductor and AI strategy with more than $576 billion in investment. As part of an 800 trillion won (~$518 billion) project, Samsung and SK Hynix will each build two new fabs in the country's southwest; Samsung has already named its site — Gwangju — while SK Hynix is taking time to choose a location.

**Technical details:** The bet is on HBM (high-bandwidth memory), where both companies hold a dominant share of the global market and serve as a critical node for all top-tier AI accelerators. An additional ~81 trillion won goes toward an advanced-packaging cluster in the Chungcheong region near Seoul. Packaging and HBM, not the GPU dies themselves, are the industry's main "bottleneck" right now.

**My take:** This isn't "just another state-support plan," but an attempt to cement a structural monopoly. HBM is the place where Korea is genuinely irreplaceable, and the government is rightly investing where it already has leverage, rather than trying to catch up to TSMC's logic from scratch. What troubles me here is something else: concentrating the world's memory production in two companies of a single country is a systemic risk for the entire industry, and any disruption (geopolitics, a natural disaster, a fab fire, as has happened before) hits everyone at once. For engineers and infrastructure architects, the practical takeaway is unpleasant: HBM shortages and prices over the next 2–3 years will be determined by political decisions in Seoul, not by market competition. Plan capacity with a buffer on lead times.

## 3. DirtyClone (CVE-2026-43503): quiet root in the Linux kernel, and the PoC is already public

**What happened:** JFrog Security Research published a working exploit for DirtyClone — a local privilege escalation to root in the Linux kernel (CVSS 8.8). It's a variant of the DirtyFrag/Fragnesia family: a bug in `__pskb_copy_fclone()`, where cloning a network packet drops the protective `SKBFL_SHARED_FRAG` flag.

**Technical details:** The attack goes through the XFRM/IPsec subsystem and modifies the page cache — the in-memory copy of a file, without touching the binary on disk. As a result, file-integrity monitoring (on-disk hashing) shows "clean," and the kernel audit logs stay empty. Affected are Debian, Fedora, and Ubuntu when unprivileged user namespaces are enabled. The fix has been in mainline since May 21 (Linux v7.1-rc5); the mitigation is `kernel.unprivileged_userns_clone=0`.

**My take:** The DirtyFrag family itself worries me more than a single CVE: we're already seeing the third or fourth variant of the same class of bugs around skb cloning, and that tells me the original architectural defense was patched pointwise rather than systemically. The most dangerous thing here is the invisibility: the exploit leaves no traces on disk or in the logs, meaning classic EDR/FIM won't save you. JFrog's public PoC means the window to "patch quietly" has closed — now it's a race. If you have unprivileged user namespaces enabled in prod (and on modern distros they're enabled by default) and you're not yet on 7.1 — turn namespaces off with the sysctl right now, without waiting for a maintenance window. This is one of those cases where a temporary kludge is justified.

## 4. AI coding breaks Azure: Microsoft drags GitHub onto AWS, its competitor

**What happened:** Microsoft is reportedly adding AWS capacity to host GitHub — after a series of outages tied to capacity exhaustion on its own Azure. The cause is explosive load growth from AI assistants: AI workloads already consume over 60% of all of GitHub's compute resources, versus ~15% at the start of 2023.

**Technical details:** GitHub weathered a three-hour outage in October 2025 and a 12-hour degradation in February 2026 at peak Copilot usage. COO Kyle Daigle estimates the commit rate at 14 billion for 2026, versus 1 billion in 2025. Microsoft calls this a multi-cloud strategy while simultaneously "accelerating" the migration to Azure.

**My take:** This is the most telling news of the week, and it's more important than any release. A company that owns Azure, GitHub, and Copilot is forced to go cap in hand to a direct competitor because its own cloud can't handle the demand it created itself. This is a public admission that the economics of "burn tokens on every whim" run up not against money, but against the physics of data centers. For the industry the signal is twofold: first, AI assistants are not a "feature on the side" but a dominant line item in the compute budget, and that needs to be factored into infrastructure planning in earnest; second, multi-cloud is ceasing to be a pretty word from slide decks and becoming a matter of a service's survival. It's ironic that the best advertisement for AWS's maturity is Microsoft's forced choice.

## 5. Comcast splits up: the pipe on one side, content on the other

**What happened:** On June 29, Comcast announced a split into two independent public companies through a tax-free spin-off of NBCUniversal and Sky. Comcast will remain — broadband access, mobile, enterprise connectivity, and network infrastructure; the media giant NBCUniversal + Sky will go its own way. The deal is expected to close in about a year.

**Technical details:** The logic is to separate the valuations of two fundamentally different business models: stable "utility" telecom infrastructure and a volatile content business. Co-CEO Mike Cavanagh will head NBCUniversal, and former CFO Michael Angelakis the infrastructure-focused Comcast.

**My take:** For an IT audience this is more interesting than it looks at first glance: the market is voting for the idea that owning "the pipe" (networks, broadband, edge infrastructure) is a standalone, predictable, and clearly valued asset that is only burdened by being tied to a capricious media business. This is a continuation of a big trend: telecom infrastructure is increasingly viewed as a utility on the level of power grids. In the context of the AI boom and demand for capacity and low latency, a pure connectivity player without media ballast gains the freedom to invest in edge and backbone for new workloads. A skeptical note: spin-offs are often sold as "unlocking value," but in practice they are frequently a way to elegantly write off a problematic segment — and media here is clearly the one being shed, not the one being given wings.
