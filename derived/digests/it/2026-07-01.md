## 1. IREN buys Mirantis for $625M: OpenStack and Kubernetes move under a GPU cloud

**What happened:** Cloud provider IREN announced the acquisition of Mirantis for $625 million. Mirantis remains an independent subsidiary, and the deal aims to unify the management of OpenStack, Kubernetes, and GPU clusters under one roof.

**Technical details:** Mirantis brings a mature private-cloud stack (k0s, Lens, Mirantis Kubernetes Engine) and expertise in bare-metal orchestration, which IREN wants to stretch over its GPU farms for AI workloads.

By buying Mirantis, IREN is trying to stop being merely a socket for graphics cards and become a platform. ## 2. etcd 3.7.0 beta: RangeStream finally arrives at the heart of Kubernetes

**What happened:** On June 26, SIG-Etcd released the first beta of etcd v3.7.0 — the distributed key-value store on which every Kubernetes cluster's control plane rests. The release's key feature is the long-awaited RangeStream, plus a large-scale refactor and code cleanup.

**Technical details:** RangeStream lets you stream large key ranges in chunks instead of one giant Range response, relieving peak memory pressure and reducing the risk of OOM on large clusters with tens of thousands of objects.

**My take:** etcd is one of those components that everyone uses and no one wants to touch, because the cost of a mistake is a downed control plane for an entire cluster. RangeStream addresses a very real pain: on large installations, list requests to etcd regularly cause cascading failures when apiserver pulls everything at once. The fact that the feature was dragged along for years shows how conservatively (and rightly) the team treats changes to the foundation. For platform engineers, this is a reason to look closely at the beta on staging: the memory win could prove decisive where etcd previously had to be sharded or artificially throttled. But into prod — only after a stable release; haste is inadmissible here.

## 3. SimpleHelp CVE-2026-48558: CVSS 10, RMM server as a skeleton key, and already in KEV

**What happened:** A vulnerability, CVE-2026-48558, with a maximum CVSS score of 10.0 has been found in the SimpleHelp RMM platform. An attacker was able to obtain a trusted technician session on an internet-exposed SimpleHelp server; after Blackpoint published its analysis, CISA added the bug to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on June 29.

**Technical details:** The hole grants an unauthenticated takeover of a "technician session" — that is, full trusted access to managed machines, including remote command execution on all connected endpoints.

**My take:** RMM platforms are an ideal target, any attacker's dream: a single compromised server grants fan-out access to all of an MSP's client machines. We've already seen this with Kaseya and ScreenConnect, and SimpleHelp fits squarely into the same supply-chain-through-administration-tools pattern. A CVSS 10 without authentication on an internet-facing service isn't a "patch it this week," it's a "shut off access right now and check the logs for a breach that already happened." Landing in KEV means US federal agencies are required to close the hole on tight deadlines, but the real mass of victims is thousands of small MSPs with SimpleHelp sticking out to the internet without segmentation. The lesson repeats year after year: a remote management tool should not be reachable from the open internet, period.

## 4. Oracle EBS under fire: CVE-2026-46817 reads files without a password, honeypots already catching attacks

**What happened:** A critical vulnerability, CVE-2026-46817, has been found in Oracle E-Business Suite's Oracle Payments module — network-based, unauthenticated, exploitable over plain HTTP, and leading to compromise of the payment module. The Defused project reported that its EBS honeypots recorded unauthenticated file-read attempts through this hole on June 27.

**Technical details:** The vulnerability allows reading arbitrary files on the server without authorization via the Payments HTTP endpoint — a classic path to leaking configs, credentials, and private keys, and from there to a full takeover.

**My take:** Oracle EBS is that legacy mastodon quietly running finance and procurement at thousands of large companies, and that's precisely why such bugs are especially dangerous: they're rarely patched promptly, because "if it works, don't touch it," and an ERP maintenance window takes months to coordinate. The fact that exploitation was caught by honeypots almost immediately after disclosure says: attackers monitor Oracle advisories no worse than we do and deploy scanners in the very first days. For engineers this is a reminder that the perimeter isn't only trendy microservices, but also the old monolith on WebLogic that everyone forgot about. If your EBS faces outward — it's the first candidate for lockdown and audit, and it should have been done yesterday.
