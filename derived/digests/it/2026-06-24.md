## 1. China's LineShine tears up the TOP500: 2.2 exaflops and, for the first time, CPU-only

**What happened:** On June 23, at the ISC High Performance conference in Hamburg, the 67th TOP500 list was published. Bursting suddenly into first place was the previously unlisted Chinese supercomputer LineShine, displacing El Capitan. The system delivered 2.198 exaflops on the High Performance Linpack benchmark — the first computer in TOP500 history to cross two sustained double-precision exaflops, and to do it exclusively on CPUs, with no GPU accelerators.

**Technical details:** 2.198 EF/s is about 80% of the theoretical peak of 2.736 EF/s, which speaks to high efficiency (HPL efficiency) for a pure processor machine. There are now five systems above one exaflops on the list; the European JUPITER Booster (EuroHPC, Jülich) holds 5th place with exactly 1.000 EF/s as Europe's only exascale machine.

**My take:** A CPU-only exaflops is the detail worth reading the list closely for, rather than skimming the top line. In recent years we've been sold the narrative that "there's nothing to do in HPC without GPUs," yet China shows that for classic double-precision Linpack a dense processor cluster with a good interconnect is still competitive — and, as a bonus, it sidesteps export restrictions on accelerators. An 80% HPL efficiency at two exaflops is strong engineering in network topology and memory balance, not just "piling on cores." For those who live in HPC, the main practical question is not the peak figures but how friendly such an architecture is to real workloads and toolchains: Linpack loves CPUs, but MPI applications with intensive communication are a completely different story. And yes, the opacity around Chinese systems (where it's located, what it's built on) still gets in the way of believing the figures one hundred percent.

## 2. Miasma: a worm in Red Hat npm packages forges SLSA provenance

**What happened:** Researchers at Wiz, followed by Microsoft and Red Hat, disclosed a major supply chain attack (codenamed Miasma — "creeping infection") on packages in the @redhat-cloud-services npm namespace. At least 32 releases contained unauthorized modifications that don't match the sources in the repositories; in aggregate these packages drew about 80,000 downloads a week. The root cause — a compromised GitHub account of a Red Hat employee, through which orphan commits were pushed in bypassing code review.

**Technical details:** The attacker triggered GitHub Actions, requested OIDC tokens, and published trojanized packages with valid SLSA provenance. On installation, a preinstall hook launched an obfuscated 4.29 MB dropper: in CI/CD it scraped secrets from the memory of the GitHub Actions runner, escalated privileges via passwordless sudo, and re-published the poisoned packages with forged provenance — that is, it spread like a worm. The TTPs echo the "Mini Shai-Hulud" campaign.

**My take:** The most unpleasant thing here is not the fact of compromise (accounts get stolen constantly), but that the attack forges SLSA provenance, which the industry has spent the last few years selling as the antidote to exactly such attacks. When the "proof of origin" itself becomes forged, the whole trust model for the supply chain collapses: the signature is there, the provenance is green, and inside there's a worm. The lesson for everyone building pipelines: OIDC tokens in GitHub Actions and passwordless sudo on runners are not a convenience but a loaded gun, and npm preinstall hooks have long needed to be kept behind bars (at minimum --ignore-scripts by default, CI isolation, short-lived credentials). Provenance is a necessary but categorically insufficient condition; you should trust not the badge but the reproducibility of the build.

**My take:** This is a purchase not of set-top boxes but of the television's operating system and the ad stack beneath it — and that's the whole point. Fox is essentially admitting that in the streaming era, distribution and viewer data matter more than content production, and instead of building its own CTV platform from scratch, it buys a ready one with tens of millions of devices. For the industry, this is yet another signal of consolidation: the "pipe" (delivery platform) and the "water" (content) are once again fusing in the same hands, as in the days of cable monopolies, only now on the basis of an OS and ad auctions.

## 4. HPE Slingshot 400: a 400 Gbps interconnect for the exaflops era

**What happened:** At ISC 2026 in Hamburg, HPE unveiled the Slingshot Interconnect 400 — a new generation of its proprietary HPC interconnect at 400 Gbps. It is available for the HPE Supercomputing GX5000 platform, as well as in the form of a top-of-rack switch with PCIe network cards, and is positioned as a network for exaflops systems.

**Technical details:** Doubling the bandwidth from 200 to 400 Gbps per port, plus the ToR-switch variant with a PCIe NIC, means Slingshot stops being tied exclusively to Cray's proprietary blade chassis and enters more standard rack builds. For HPC, what's critical is not just the gigabits but the adaptive routing and congestion control for which Slingshot is renowned on tight MPI traffic.

**My take:** If LineShine from the first story shows that CPU clusters are alive, then Slingshot 400 explains why: at exascale the bottleneck has long been the network rather than the flops, and whoever wins the interconnect wins the real performance of applications. Doubling the bandwidth is expected, but what's truly important is the move to a ToR switch and PCIe cards: HPE is decoupling Slingshot from Cray's proprietary chassis and trying to sell it as a network for a broader market, competing with NVIDIA's InfiniBand NDR/XDR and Ultra Ethernet. For those who build and maintain the HPC stack, this means, going forward, new drivers, libfabric/OFI providers, and nodes in the EasyBuild/Spack build matrix — that is, yet another compatibility layer to validate. Competition in interconnects is the best thing that could happen to HPC, since the InfiniBand monoculture has kept prices high for years.

## 5. Rust 1.96.0: Copy ranges and a hard error on undefined symbols in WebAssembly

**What happened:** Rust 1.96.0 was released — the next stable release of the language. The main innovation is a new set of Range types that implement Copy: per RFC3550, ranges now implement IntoIterator instead of Iterator directly, which allows them to be Copy. Also, two new assert macros were stabilized and two vulnerabilities (CVE-2026-5223 and CVE-2026-5222) were closed for users of third-party crate registries; they do not affect crates.io users.

**Technical details:** The most important thing in practice is a breaking change for WebAssembly: as of 1.96, undefined symbols in wasm targets became a hard linker error, whereas previously they were silently turned into imports. Plus, passing the expr metavariable into cfg was allowed and never-type coercion in tuple expressions was refined.

**My take:** Copy for ranges is one of those small things that annoyed for years anyone who ever tried to pass a Range into a closure and caught a "value moved." Fixing it by switching the trait from Iterator to IntoIterator is a neat example of how Rust sorts out early design decisions without breaking compatibility head-on. But what really deserves attention is precisely WebAssembly: turning undefined symbols into a hard error is the right but painful step, which will break builds for those who implicitly relied on auto-imports, and those builds will fall apart silently when the toolchain is upgraded. If you have wasm targets in CI, budget time to sort out linking before updating to 1.96. Overall this is a release without loud features, and that's good: a mature language should move in small, calibrated steps rather than revolutions every six months.
