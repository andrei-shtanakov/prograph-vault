## 1. Qualcomm buys Modular for $3.9B: flanking Nvidia's CUDA crowd through software

**What happened:** On June 24, Qualcomm officially confirmed the acquisition of AI software startup Modular for roughly $3.9B in an all-stock deal (up to 19.2 million new shares), and by June 26 the deal was already being described as closed. The main asset is not silicon but the software layer: the Mojo language and the MAX inference engine, which allow running models on the hardware of various vendors (Nvidia, AMD, Intel, and others) without rewriting code. Some 150 employees and co-founders Chris Lattner (creator of LLVM and Swift) and Tim Davis are moving to Qualcomm.

**Technical details:** MAX + Mojo is, in essence, an attempt to build a vendor-neutral alternative to CUDA: a single compiler and runtime that abstract GPUs/accelerators so that the same code ports between platforms. The deal's close is expected in the second half of 2026 after regulatory and shareholder approvals.

**My take:** This is a purchase of a compiler, not a team, and therein lies its main meaning. Everyone understands that Nvidia's technological advantage is not so much the silicon as CUDA: ten years of accumulated libraries, toolchains, and developer muscle memory that cannot be copied in a quarter. Qualcomm is buying not "yet another framework" but the person who has already once built LLVM and Swift — that is, who knows how ecosystems are made, not individual tools. My skepticism is hard: "hardware-agnostic compute" has been promised by many (oneAPI, ROCm, SYCL, Triton), and nearly all of them crashed against reality — portability on paper does not equal performance on specific hardware, and CUDA wins precisely on the last mile of optimization. But the direction is strategically right: as long as there's one dominant software moat, the market will pay for any plausible attempt to breach it. For us who work with HPC and build systems, the key question is whether Mojo/MAX will arrive in normal package managers and toolchains, or remain yet another vendor island.

## 2. CISA adds Ubiquiti UniFi OS and Lantronix to KEV at once: edge and OT under root again

**What happened:** On June 23, CISA added several actively exploited holes in edge devices to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog at once. In Ubiquiti UniFi OS — a chain of three maximum-critical bugs (CVE-2026-34908, CVE-2026-34909, CVE-2026-34910) yielding unauthenticated remote command execution; it is already being used to deploy commodity malware. In parallel — CVE-2025-67038 in the Lantronix EDS5000 OT converter (part of the BRIDGE:BREAK set from Forescout), allowing an unauthenticated attacker to execute arbitrary commands with root privileges. Under directive BOD 26-04, federal agencies were given three days to remediate.

**Technical details:** In UniFi, the chain glues together an NGINX-gateway authentication bypass (CVE-2026-34908/34909) with improper input validation (CVE-2026-34910) — a classic bypass→injection. In the Lantronix EDS5000, the HTTP RPC module does not sanitize the username parameter before concatenating it into the shell command that logs failed logins: OS command injection via the username. Exploitation of Lantronix was recorded in a honeypot as early as April 5 — attackers apparently reverse-engineered the patch.

**My take:** The plot repeats along exactly the same lines as FortiBleed and Cisco SD-WAN from previous issues, and this is not a coincidence but a structural disease: edge boxes — routers, Wi-Fi controllers, OT converters — are closed "black boxes" with their own firmware that sit at the perimeter, face the internet, and are patched last. Lantronix worries me more than UniFi: a serial-to-IP converter is a communication chokepoint of industrial automation, and root on it is a potential loss of control over physical processes, not a data leak. Command injection via the username field is a bug that's shameful by today's standards, one that should not survive a single code review in 2026, but in the embedded world such things live for decades. The practical takeaway is familiar and therefore painful: inventory edge and OT devices as critical infrastructure, not as "a box that just works," and assume that a patch after public exploitation is only half the work; the other half is hunting for already-installed backdoors.

**My take:** What's curious here is not the sum but that Nvidia sits on both sides of the deal — it invests in Reflection and sells the chips that Reflection rents from SpaceX. For the industry, something else matters more — SpaceX has quietly become a compute landlord, and this is a signal that compute has finally become an exchange-traded commodity: some build it, others own it, still others rent it. $150M a month with a 90-day exit is not a capital build but renting an apartment, and such liquidity of capacity is good for startups (no need to freeze billions in hardware) but systemically dangerous: the whole tower rests on the music not stopping. ## 4. TypeScript 7.0 RC: the compiler was rewritten in Go and became ~10x faster

**What happened:** Microsoft released the Release Candidate of TypeScript 7.0 — a version where the compiler is rewritten from TypeScript itself into Go (the tsgo project). A type-checking speedup of roughly 10x is claimed: the 1.5-million-line VS Code project builds in 7.5 seconds instead of 77.8. The RC is already available on npm; the stable release is promised in about a month.

**Technical details:** This is a port, not a rewrite: the team deliberately carried over the same algorithms and data structures, preserving identical type-checking semantics. About half of the gain comes from native code (no V8 JIT overhead and no Node.js startup), the other half from parallelism that was unavailable to the old single-threaded runtime. Parallel builds, a reworked watch mode, and a side-by-side mode with 6.0 were added.

**My take:** This is a rare case where "rewritten in another language" is not hype but an engineering-honest decision with a measurable result. I especially like the discipline of "a port, not a redesign": the temptation to also "fix" the type semantics along the way would have been enormous, and it's precisely that which usually kills such migrations — but here they deliberately carried over the same bugs and the same corners so as not to break millions of existing code bases. Cutting build time from 78 to 7 seconds is not cosmetics but a change of tool class: at such figures the very development cycle changes, watch becomes instant, CI gets cheaper. The choice of Go instead of Rust for a CPU-bound compiler will be criticized by some, but the pragmatics are obvious — a fast GC language with excellent parallelism and a low contribution barrier, which for a project of this scale matters more than the last few percent of performance. For everyone with large TS monorepos: the RC can already be run alongside stable 6.0, and I'd start running CI on tsgo right now, so that by GA you know your pitfalls.

## 5. Cloudflare, Azure, and AWS went down almost simultaneously: the internet's perimeter cracks again

**What happened:** On June 22 a broad wave of outages rolled through: X, Reddit, Microsoft Teams, Zoom, Canva, Discord, Fortnite, and a number of AWS-dependent services fell at once. Trackers showed synchronous spikes, Cloudflare reported elevated errors and latency across several services; for some users it eased in about 20 minutes. Analysts noted an alarming pattern — a clustering of outages at Cloudflare, Azure, and AWS within a close time window.

**Technical details:** The symptoms were degradation, not a full outage: elevated latency and a wave of 5XX, typical of problems at the edge/CDN and DNS level, when it is not a single site that falls but the common layer through which hundreds of services pass. It is precisely the concentration of traffic on three or four providers that turns a local incident into "the internet is down."

**My take:** Technically a 20-minute degradation is a trifle, but the news is not about the specific outage, it's about the pattern: the internet effectively rests on three companies, and when any of them sneezes, half the visible web goes down. For years we designed fault tolerance inside our own systems — replicas, availability zones, multi-region — and yet sat the whole stack on one Cloudflare and one AWS, zeroing out all that work with a single external dependency. The clustering of outages at three giants in one window is not a conspiracy theory but a regularity of overheated, over-complicated, and over-centralized infrastructure, where shared dependencies (one DNS provider, one CDN, one BGP upstream) create hidden correlations of failure. The practical takeaway is inconvenient and expensive: real multi-cloud and multi-CDN is not paranoia but hygiene for everything that's critical, and if your business dies together with Cloudflare, then you have not an architecture but a bet. 2026 keeps hinting more insistently that outages of this scale are the new normal, not an anomaly.
