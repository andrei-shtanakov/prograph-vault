## 1. Miasma / Shai-Hulud Wave 3: the npm worm learned to live inside AI assistants and survive `rm -rf node_modules`

**What happened:** The Shai-Hulud wave of supply-chain attacks has reached a new level: the Miasma variant (based on the public Mini Shai-Hulud codebase from the TeamPCP group, released in mid-May) has been self-replicating across npm since June 1, and in the first hours it was simultaneously spotted by Wiz, Snyk, Aikido, and Socket. Infected are 32 packages and 96 versions in Red Hat's `@redhat-cloud-services` namespace, dozens of Microsoft repositories on GitHub, and on June 24 `codfish/semantic-release-action` was hijacked (tags v2–v5 redirected to steal CI/CD secrets).

**Technical details:** The main novelty is not install-time execution but persistence in the AI developer's toolchain. Miasma writes backdoors into `.claude/settings.json` (SessionStart hook), `.cursor/rules/setup.mdc`, `.gemini/settings.json`, `.vscode/tasks.json` (runs on folder open), plus `.github/setup.js` and malicious MCP servers. The payload re-launches every time the developer opens the project in an AI IDE, and survives `npm uninstall` and a full `node_modules` cleanup. Its arsenal includes SLSA-provenance forgery (passes `npm audit`), extraction of OIDC tokens from GitHub Actions runner memory, and a DEADMAN_SWITCH that wipes the machine when tokens are revoked, before network isolation.

**My take:** This, not yet another CVE in a piece of hardware, is the truly alarming shift of the year. The classic supply-chain model breached you on `npm install`; Miasma breaches you on "open the project in Cursor." The attackers grasped a simple thing: AI-assistant configs are executable code with autorun that no one reviews and that lives outside `node_modules` — that is, outside the zone auditors look at and dependency cleanups hit. The SLSA-provenance forgery is especially infuriating: the industry sold provenance for years as the solution to the trust problem, and it turns out attestation can be forged, and `npm audit` shows green. The practical takeaway is harsh: treat `.claude/`, `.cursor/`, `.gemini/`, `.vscode/tasks.json`, and the list of MCP servers as executable artifacts — version them, review them in PRs, and monitor them for changes on par with code. And stop believing that deleting `node_modules` cleans you — that's no longer true.

## 2. SharePoint under active attack: CVE-2026-45659 in KEV, feds given until July 4

**What happened:** CISA added Microsoft SharePoint Server vulnerability CVE-2026-45659 (CVSS 8.8) to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog — an RCE via deserialization of untrusted data, with exploitation recorded in the wild. Microsoft released a patch back in May for SharePoint Server Subscription Edition, 2019, and Enterprise 2016, but US federal agencies (FCEB) have been given a mandatory deadline to close it — July 4, 2026.

**Technical details:** Deserialization-of-untrusted-data is the very class of bug that turns SharePoint into an entry point time and again: a gadget chain yields remote code execution on the server, then lateral movement across the corporate network. SharePoint historically sits on the perimeter, is integrated with AD, and stores internal documents, so a single RCE scales into a full domain compromise.

**My take:** What matters here is not the vulnerability itself but the timing: weeks passed between the May patch and the KEV listing, and that's exactly the window where most victims live — "we'll patch on the scheduled maintenance window." SharePoint is a corporate mastodon administered on a residual basis, with downtime coordinated for months, and attackers know this perfectly well. A CISA deadline of a couple of days is a signal that exploitation is not hypothetical but mass: FCEB mandatory deadlines are usually set when they already see the scans and breaches. If your SharePoint faces outward and you're still on the May version without the patch — you're not "on the list to update," you're already in the zone of possible compromise, and the first thing to do is not patch but check the logs for a breach that already happened.

## 3. TOP500: China's LineShine debuts at number one with 2.198 EFlop/s and dethrones El Capitan

El Capitan (LLNL) is bumped to #2 (1.809 EFlop/s), Frontier (ORNL) to #3 (1.353 EFlop/s). By number of systems, the US leads (161), followed by Japan (44) and Germany (41).

**Technical details:** LineShine posted exaflop-class HPL numbers "out of nowhere" — without prior public benchmarks, which is typical of Chinese machines that for years were not submitted to the list due to export geopolitics. A 20% lead over El Capitan is not an increment but a bid for a change of leader, and the question now is how well these peak HPL figures convert into real efficiency on application codes.

**Technical details for the engineer:** What's genuinely interesting is not the #1 line but the fact that China has again started showing top-tier systems officially — this changes the picture of the exaflop race, which in recent years was shaped mainly by DOE machines on AMD (El Capitan, Frontier). For those assembling easyconfigs and toolchains for HPC, a change of leader usually also means a change of the reference architecture that libraries are optimized for: if #1 is again on a closed Chinese stack, part of the community's optimizations will bypass the mainstream ROCm/CUDA paths. I'd keep skepticism toward bare HPL: 2.198 EFlop/s is LINPACK, not HPCG and not real simulations, and the gap on application workloads is almost always smaller than the showcase number. But symbolically this is an important moment — the DOE monopoly at the top of the list is over, and the "US vs China" duopoly in exaflops has become real again.

## 4. CPUs in shortage again: Xeon sold out for all of 2026, and the CPU:GPU ratio in inference heads toward 1:1

**What happened:** Intel confirmed that it's shifting capacity from consumer chips to server Xeons: Xeon production lines are sold out for 2026, server-CPU lead times hold around six months, and "unmet demand" is called "significant." The cause is the shift of AI workloads from training to inference: per Intel's estimate from Q1-2026, in agentic scenarios the data-center CPU-to-GPU ratio could tighten from the current 1:4–1:8 to 1:1.

**Technical details:** CPUs like Granite Rapids are ceasing to be the "harness" around GPUs and becoming a critical bottleneck: they orchestrate distributed inference, run preprocessing, routing, and agentic loops, where each GPU needs more and more host. Server CPUs have risen 10–20% since March, consumer ones 5–10%, and analysts expect another +8–10% in the second half of the year.

**My take:** We spent years saying "AI infrastructure = GPU," and now the surprise — the bottleneck once again becomes the host processor. This is exactly the class of news that hits planning, not slides: if you're speccing an HPC or inference cluster for 2026, six-month lead times on Xeon and +20% on price are no longer a risk but a given, and capacity must be reserved with a buffer on timelines. The 1:1 logic in agentic scenarios seems plausible to me: an agent isn't "one forward pass" but dozens of steps with tool-calls, orchestration, and branching, and all of that churns precisely on the CPU. The irony is that the accelerator boom returned value to the most boring component in the rack — and those who spent the last two years cutting CPU planning out of the budget in favor of GPUs are now catching a shortage from both sides at once. The practical takeaway: treat CPUs as just as scarce a resource as HBM, and put them into the cluster spec first, not on a residual basis.
