# IT Digest — June 29, 2026

A roundup of the 5 most significant IT news stories of the past few days.

## 1. Klue: one forgotten integration took down the Salesforce data of half the security industry

**What happened:** A supply-chain attack through the competitive-intelligence platform Klue resulted in the theft of Salesforce data from dozens of organizations, and the list of victims includes a whole stack of well-known security vendors: HackerOne, Huntress, Snyk, Tanium, Recorded Future, OneTrust, Jamf, Gong. The Icarus group listed Klue on its leak site on June 19 and is sending victims emails with a 48-hour ultimatum. On June 11, Salesforce disabled the Klue Battlecards integration in response to the incident.

**Technical details:** The entry point was a long-abandoned service account that Klue once created for a prototype of a third-party integration and forgot to deactivate. Through it, the attackers pushed a malicious code update that harvested OAuth tokens, and then used those very tokens to siphon CRM data from clients' connected Salesforce instances.

**My take:** This is a textbook on what a modern supply chain looks like in practice: you get breached not through your code but through a third party to which you once issued a token and forgot about it. It is especially bitter that among the victims are companies that themselves sell protection: if HackerOne and Huntress didn't keep track of an OAuth integration's scope, then an ordinary business has even less chance of noticing such a thing. The main takeaway for engineers — OAuth tokens and service accounts must have a lifetime and audit on par with passwords, and a "temporary integration for a prototype" must automatically die. As long as the industry treats tokens as something you issue and forget, such cascades will keep repeating. And yes, your company's real attack surface is the sum of the surfaces of all the SaaS you gave access to.

## 2. Tata Electronics confirmed a 630 GB leak: Apple and Tesla blueprints went to the dark web

**What happened:** Tata Electronics — an Indian assembler accounting for around 30% of global iPhone production — officially confirmed a cyberattack on part of its IT infrastructure on June 22. Back on June 10, the World Leaks group had posted 630 GB of data (over 204,000 files): Apple board-inspection documents, Tesla Model 3 Highland blueprints marked trade secret, emails, logs, and copies of employee passports.

**Technical details:** The leak is not about a hole in a protocol but about a classic double extortion — exfiltrating data before extortion. The danger here is not in the files themselves but in the fact that supplier specifications and production tolerances fell into the wrong hands, from which the details of clients' product lines can be reconstructed.

**My take:** Apple spent years building diversification away from Foxconn and China, and Tata was a key element of that plan — and now it turns out that moving production to a new geography automatically drags along a new risk perimeter. This is a reminder that the "hardware" supply chain is just as vulnerable as the software supply chain: your most valuable secrets sit not with you but with a third-tier contractor whose SOC may be weaker than yours. For the industry this is an argument that contractual cybersecurity requirements for suppliers must be as strict as quality requirements for soldering. A contractor assembling your flagship is also a repository of your trade secrets, whether you like it or not.

## 3. Linux 7.1: a new NTFS driver, Intel FRED by default, and 140,000 lines of legacy gone

**What happened:** On June 14, the stable Linux 7.1 kernel was released. The main highlight is a new NTFS implementation that was worked on for four years: full-fledged writing with delayed allocation, iomap, and folio integration, plus a fresh set of ntfsprogs-plus utilities. The merge window absorbed almost 13,000 commits from 2,011 developers, 342 of whom submitted their first patch.

**Technical details:** On Intel machines, FRED (Flexible Return and Event Delivery) is enabled by default — a cleaner mechanism for handling transitions between privilege levels. The 486-era x86 sub-architectures were removed (−140,000 lines), Landlock for UNIX sockets by path was added, and, on the operationally painful side, UDP Lite and the module mode of IPv6 were cut, which will break custom configs in production.

**My take:** The main value of 7.1 is not the new features but what the kernel is getting rid of. Removing 140,000 lines for dead hardware and dropping UDP Lite is healthy discipline: less code, less attack surface, less that has to be maintained forever. The new NTFS driver is also long-awaited — the old paragon/ntfs3 was a source of eternal pain, and normal writing with folio integration will genuinely make life easier for those who live in a heterogeneous Windows/Linux environment. But the removal of UDP Lite and IPv6 module mode is a reason not to roll out 7.1 into production blindly: check your configs in advance, or you'll catch a surprise on reboot. Overall this is a release I like for its maturity: the kernel is growing up and isn't afraid to cut away its past.

## 4. PostgreSQL 19 Beta 1: a bet on logical replication and observability

**What happened:** On June 4, the first beta of PostgreSQL 19 was released — a preview of major improvements in performance, developer experience, security, monitoring, and, above all, logical replication. This is the start of the testing cycle before the stable release, which traditionally comes out by autumn.

**Technical details:** The emphasis is on logical replication (important for near-zero-downtime upgrades and multi-master scenarios) and on expanded observability — more built-in metrics and statistics to diagnose bottlenecks without third-party extensions.

**My take:** PostgreSQL long ago stopped being "just a database" and turned into the default choice for a new backend, so each major here is an event for the whole industry, not just for DBAs. The emphasis on logical replication is the most pragmatic vector: it is precisely this that remains a pain point in upgrades and migrations of large installations, and improvements here convert directly into less downtime. Built-in observability is also the right trend: the more a database can tell about itself, the less need there is to cobble together exotica from extensions and external exporters. A practical piece of advice: a beta is an invitation to run your load tests now, rather than complain about regressions after the release. Postgres is strong precisely because the community really does test the betas rather than wait for the weather at the seaside.

## 5. RISC-V matured into the server: Server Platform Spec 1.0 ratified, Ubuntu picks up RVA23

**What happened:** RISC-V officially formalized the Server Platform Specification 1.0 based on the RVA23 profile, and Canonical declared full support for RVA23 in Ubuntu 26.04 LTS. Against this backdrop, server chips rained down: SiFive Performance P870D (up to 128 cores), NextSilicon Arbel, Akeana Alpine, Epic Semi Contrail AIX (32 RISC-V cores + 16 AI cores, up to 75 TOPS). NVIDIA is porting CUDA components to RISC-V and integrating NVLink Fusion.

**Technical details:** The Server Platform Spec sets a single base contract (boot, interrupts, device discovery), without which the same distribution would not run on chips from different vendors. RVA23 as a profile fixes the mandatory set of ISA extensions — that is, software can finally be built for a predictable target rather than a zoo of optional features.

**My take:** This is a less loud story than a breach or a new kernel, but in the long run — potentially the most important in the roundup. An architecture without a standard server platform is doomed to remain a niche: it was precisely the absence of a predictable base contract that kept RISC-V in the embedded ghetto for many years. Spec 1.0 plus RVA23 in Ubuntu LTS is the moment when the ecosystem moves from "an interesting experiment" to "you can plan production." That NVIDIA is dragging CUDA and NVLink Fusion onto RISC-V is the strongest signal: it means big players see a real market here, not an open-ISA ideology. I'd hold back the enthusiasm for now — between the spec's ratification and a mature server ecosystem lie years of work on toolchains, drivers, and enterprise trust. But for the first time in a long while, RISC-V has a clear route into the data center rather than just a manifesto.
