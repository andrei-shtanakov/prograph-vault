## 1. Oracle PeopleSoft zero-day bursts the dam: ShinyHunters carry off Nissan, NAIC, Medtronic, and Aflac

**What happened:** The ShinyHunters campaign around a zero-day in Oracle PeopleSoft in the week of June 26 – July 2 spilled into a cascade of disclosures. Nissan confirmed a leak of current and former employee data in the US, Canada, Mexico, and Brazil (down to SSNs, banking, and tax data). The US insurance regulator NAIC admitted the theft of 3.1 TB (~105,000 files), Medtronic is notifying of the compromise of 9+ million records with "health-related" data, and Aflac reported a leak through its Japanese subsidiary.

**Technical details:** The vector is exploitation of a zero-day in the Oracle PeopleSoft server, not phishing: a single hole in the ERP/HCM platform grants access to HR data across many organizations at once. This is not the story of the early-July EBS vulnerability (CVE-2026-46817) but a separate PeopleSoft front — the same Oracle stack, but a different product and a different compromise chain.

**My take:** What frightens me here is not the size of each individual leak but that this is a classic supply chain through shared enterprise software: one PeopleSoft hole — and down come a carmaker, an insurance regulator, a medtech firm, and an insurer, i.e. industries connected to each other by nothing but a single vendor. This is exactly the systemic risk I keep harping on: your security equals the security of the most riddled piece of someone else's ERP in your perimeter, and you don't control that piece. Separately, I'll note the victims' behavior — Medtronic, judging by the disappearance of its data from the ShinyHunters site, most likely paid, and that's a bad precedent that feeds the next wave. The practical takeaway for engineers is harsh: PeopleSoft and similar heavy ERPs must be kept behind a WAF and segmented as if they already have an unpatched hole, because the interval between a zero-day and mass exploitation is now shorter than the patching cycle of most enterprise teams. And if you still store passwords or PII in a PeopleSoft instance sticking out to the internet — that's no longer a risk but a matter of time.

## 2. Eight years and that's it: Google finally loses the €4.1B Android case

**What happened:** On July 2, the EU Court of Justice (CJEU) rejected Google's final appeal against the €4.1 billion antitrust fine for abuse of Android's dominance. The decision is final and not subject to appeal. This is the finale of a saga begun by the European Commission in 2018 (originally €4.34 billion, reduced by the General Court to €4.1 billion in 2022).

**Technical details:** Three practices were ruled illegal: mandatory pre-installation of Google Search and Chrome as a condition of the Play Store license, the ban on OEMs selling devices on unapproved Android forks, and revenue-sharing in exchange for exclusive pre-installation of search.

**My take:** The sum itself is a rounding error for Alphabet, but what matters is not the fine but that Brussels has rock-solidly cemented the doctrine "pre-installation as a lever of dominance." For me this isn't about Google-2018 but about how the "model + default assistant" bundle will now be judged: the very same pre-installation logic maps one-to-one onto built-in AI agents in OSes and browsers. When Microsoft dreams of a Copilot OS and Google shoves Gemini as the default into Android, this precedent is a direct warning that "making your own thing the default on your own platform" is punishable in the EU, and the DMA gives the regulator an even shorter leash. I keep skepticism about effectiveness: eight years of litigation for a fine that didn't change the business model is more a demonstration that antitrust law is hopelessly behind the speed of platforms. But the vector for us who build products in ecosystems is clear: assume that "default by default" in Europe is a regulatory liability, not a free advantage.

## 3. Nvidia turns on "compute now, pay later": GPUs on installment for a share of revenue

**What happened:** On July 3 it became known that Nvidia is rolling out a scheme under which AI clouds and startups get access to GPUs not through full prepayment but through revenue-sharing and credit support. In effect, Nvidia is turning from a chip seller into an infrastructure financier: it lends companies compute "on credit" for a share of future revenue, in order to pull more of its own capacity toward those who can't put down hundreds of millions upfront.

**Technical details:** The model strikes at the main barrier of AI infrastructure — capex: an H-series cluster costs like a small factory, and without prepayment the entry threshold for neoclouds drops sharply. In return, Nvidia locks the customer into its ecosystem for years ahead, converting a one-time hardware sale into a stream of payments.

**My take:** This, in my view, is the most underrated news of the day — and the most alarming. Nvidia is ceasing to be a vendor and becoming a vendor-financier, and that's the very pattern that in past cycles (the telecom bubble, Lucent/Nortel) ended with the manufacturer financing its own demand and drawing itself revenue on credit. The mechanics are brilliant right up until the first downturn: while AI clouds grow, revenue-sharing looks like a win-win, but at the first failure of utilization Nvidia finds itself the holder of the credit risk of an entire neocloud industry. For me as an engineer there are two takeaways. First — access to top-tier GPUs ceases to be a question of money and becomes a question of credit history and loyalty to the ecosystem, which cements CUDA lock-in even harder (and makes stories like Qualcomm/Modular from past digests even more important). Second — this is an early signal that organic capex on GPUs is no longer enough and the market has started to be fed by financial engineering; I'd read this not as demand strength but as a first hint of its ceiling. Keep your execution layer portable and don't sign up for compute that's profitable only while the music plays.

## 4. Vercel rolls out Agent Stack: open-source framework eve and Vercel Services in beta

**What happened:** At its Ship conference, Vercel presented Agent Stack, and on July 1 launched Vercel Services in beta — a platform for deploying full-stack applications. The core of the announcement is eve, an open-source, TypeScript-native framework where each agent is a directory of files that compiles into a durable production service on Vercel Functions without separate hosting infrastructure. The bundle includes AI Gateway, Sandboxes, Workflows, Connect, plus the enterprise offerings Vercel Connect and Passport (Public Beta) and BYOC on AWS (Private Beta).

**Technical details:** The key idea of eve is "agent as code in the repository": not a runtime config and not a visual builder, but ordinary files under git from which a deterministic service is assembled. This removes the main pain of agentic pipelines — reproducibility and deployment — leaving everything in the familiar dev workflow (PR, review, rollback).

**My take:** Setting aside the marketing about "agents," this is essentially about a deployment platform trying to become the standard execution environment for a new class of applications, and the "agent = directory of files" approach frankly appeals to me — it's proper engineering against the fashionable chaos of YAML orchestrators and no-code builders. Anything that returns agents to the git-native world with normal review and rollback I welcome: reproducibility matters more than yet another wrapper over an LLM call. But I keep skepticism along two lines. First — this is a very "vercel-shaped" solution: eve is formally open-source, but all the value (durable execution, Gateway, Sandboxes) is tailored to Vercel Functions, and you pay for the convenience with lock-in to their runtime and billing. Second — the agentic-framework market is already overheated (LangGraph, the fresh Claude Agent SDK, a pile of startups), and "yet another framework" has to prove not the idea but long-term support, which Vercel has historically not always delivered. Taking eve for prototypes — yes; building prod on the assumption "we'll leave Vercel when we need to" — I'd keep an abstraction on top, because the durable primitives here are proprietary, however you label them open-source.

## 5. Anthropic aims for its own silicon: talks with Samsung over a custom 2nm accelerator

**What happened:** On July 2, The Information reported that Anthropic is in preliminary talks with Samsung Electronics about manufacturing its own AI accelerator, potentially on a 2nm process and with Samsung's advanced packaging. The company has already hired specialized silicon engineers but is still defining the chip's specs, power requirements, and integration into its server clusters.

**Technical details:** This is about vertical integration: a custom ASIC for Anthropic's inference workloads instead of buying someone else's GPUs. For Samsung it's a chance to land a major foundry customer of the Tesla tier and dent TSMC's monopoly on leading-edge nodes; for Anthropic — reduced dependence on Nvidia and control over the "model ↔ hardware ↔ power consumption" bundle.

**My take:** What occupies me here is not the fact itself (everyone's building custom ASICs now — Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, Microsoft Maia) but the choice of Samsung over TSMC. If Anthropic really moves to Samsung Foundry 2nm, this is the first loud signal that the Taiwanese monopoly on leading-edge nodes has cracked — not because of quality but because of queues and geopolitics — everyone needs a second source, and Samsung has waited years for exactly this kind of anchor customer. I keep sober skepticism: "early talks" without approved specs is more often a lever in negotiations with Nvidia and TSMC than a done deal, and the path from hiring engineers to working silicon in a cluster is 2–3 years and billions, on which bigger players have stumbled. But strategically the vector is unerring, and it rhymes with news item #3: while Nvidia moves GPU access into a credit model, major labs one after another decide it's cheaper to build their own inference ASIC than to forever rent someone else's. For the industry this is a slow but inexorable shift — frontier hardware ceases to be someone else's product and becomes part of one's own stack, and the main beneficiary is the foundry diversification the market badly lacked.
