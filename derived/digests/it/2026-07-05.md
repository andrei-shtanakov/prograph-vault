## 3. Canonical puts €40,000 a year into Rust infrastructure: next target — ntpd-rs instead of classic NTP

**What happened:** Canonical became the first gold sponsor of the Trifecta Tech Foundation — the fund that rewrites system utilities in Rust (the very sudo-rs that became the default in Ubuntu). The contribution — €40,000 a year toward developing and maintaining memory-safe utilities. Next in line is ntpd-rs, a Rust implementation of a time-synchronization client and server that Ubuntu plans to ship in place of the classic daemon.

**Technical details:** Trifecta already has silver sponsors in AWS and Google. sudo-rs replaces one of the most security-sensitive binaries (setuid root); ntpd-rs targets the historically leaky NTP stack, where packet-parsing errors have more than once led to RCE and DDoS amplification.

**My take:** Rewriting core utilities in Rust has ceased to be enthusiast activism and turned into a vendor-sponsored roadmap — and that's the real news. sudo and ntpd are ideal candidates: small in code, maximal in privileges, and with a rich CVE history. €40k a year is a symbolic sum for Canonical, but it gives maintainers a predictable budget, and it's precisely burnout and the free labor of "one guy in Nebraska" that is the main vulnerability of open source. I like this vector: instead of the slogan "we'll rewrite everything in Rust," there's a targeted replacement of the most dangerous binaries, with money and an institution behind it. A cautious skeptical note — ntpd-rs still has to prove compatibility and stability in prod, but the direction is correct.

## 4. Linux 7.2 finishes off strncpy: six years and 362 commits to remove one dangerous function

**What happened:** In the Linux 7.2 merge window (Torvalds put out the first RC on June 28, with the release expected in the second half of August), the strncpy API — a source of overflows and non-null-terminated strings — was finally cut out of the kernel. The full cleanup took about six years and 362 commits. In parallel, 7.2 brought a mass of driver and subsystem improvements.

**Technical details:** strncpy is notoriously known for not guaranteeing a null terminator on truncation, which regularly yielded out-of-bounds reads. The kernel migrated to strscpy and related safe helpers; removing the final calls means the "trap" can no longer be accidentally used in new code.

**My take:** This is exactly the kind of work no one notices but that quietly makes the system safer — and I find it more appealing than any loud feature release. Six years to remove one function sounds absurd until you recall the scale: millions of lines, thousands of drivers, each call to be checked by hand. It's telling that this happens in the same month as Canonical's Rust initiatives: 2026 is shaping up as the year of systematically rooting out classes of string-handling bugs from C infrastructure — both through migration to safe APIs and through rewriting in memory-safe languages. Strategically this matters more than yet another "rc with a new Wi-Fi driver": the industry is finally treating the cause, not the symptoms.

## 5. Mageia 10 ships after three years: Wayland by default and kernel 6.18 LTS

**What happened:** On June 29–30, Mageia 10 officially shipped — a major release from the successor to Mandrake/Mandriva almost three years after Mageia 9. The distribution brings Linux kernel 6.18 LTS, Mesa 26.0, KDE Plasma 6.5.5, and GNOME 49, with Plasma and GNOME starting in a Wayland session by default for the first time in Mageia's history.

**Technical details:** The bundle includes DNF 5, GCC 15.2, Rust 1.95, Firefox 140 ESR, LibreOffice 26.2.3. Beyond the Wayland defaults, LXQt with a Wayland option is available, plus a whole set of new compositors — labwc, niri, sway, hyprland. Support for 32-bit i686 is retained (with an SSE2 requirement) — a rarity for 2026 releases.

**My take:** Mageia isn't mainstream, and yet its release is a good marker of where the whole Linux desktop is heading: Wayland has definitively become the default even at conservative, community-driven distros, while X11 moves into legacy-option status. Separately, I respect the stubborn retention of a 32-bit build — that's almost a cultural gesture in a world where even Debian is winding down i686. The practical impact on the industry here is small, the distro is niche, but as an indicator of the stack's maturity (Plasma 6.5 + Wayland "out of the box" on a volunteer project) it's quite telling. Those still keeping desktop applications on X11-specific behavior should seriously start testing under Wayland.
