## 2. Intel Xeon 6+ "Darkmont": 288 cores and a bid to return to the data center

**What happened:** Intel unveiled the Xeon 6+ server lineup on a new process node — six processors on the Darkmont core, with the flagship Xeon 6990E+ carrying 288 cores. At the same time, the company revealed details of the next generation of data-center GPUs under the codename Crescent Island, targeting AI workloads.

**Technical details:** Darkmont is an E-core (efficiency) architecture, hence the record core density: the bet is on throughput and rack density rather than single-thread. Crescent Island is positioned as a response to AMD Instinct and Nvidia in inference.

**My take:** 288 E-cores in a single socket is a direct attack on ARM servers like Ampere and Graviton, which have spent years eating into Intel's cloud segment precisely on density and energy efficiency. Intel has finally admitted that the gigahertz race in the data center is lost and is playing the density game — the right move, if a belated one. The question is whether they made it in time: for HPC and cloud providers, what matters is not peak core count but perf/watt and ecosystem maturity, and here ARM has a head start. Crescent Island, meanwhile, is already Intel's third or fourth attempt to gain a foothold in data-center GPUs, and for now you treat it with a cautious "show me the benchmarks and shipments, not the slides."

## 3. Linux 6.18 becomes the new LTS kernel

**What happened:** Linux kernel 6.18 was released, receiving long-term support status with two years of support. Among the key changes are an updated slab memory allocator and additional security hardening.

**Technical details:** The new LTS cycle means 6.18 will become the base for the next generation of enterprise distributions and embedded systems for years to come. The slab allocator improvements affect the efficiency of kernel memory management under heavy load.

**My take:** An LTS release is always an event not so much for enthusiasts as for those who plan infrastructure for years: distributions like Debian, Ubuntu LTS, and embedded systems will live on exactly this kernel. For me, the most interesting things here are the security hardening and the work on the memory allocator: the large-scale exploits of recent years have almost always come down to use-after-free and kernel memory-management problems, so each iteration on slab is a real reduction of attack surface. If you maintain an HPC or server fleet, it makes sense to look at a migration plan to 6.18 in advance rather than scramble when the current kernel reaches EOL.
