# IT Digest — June 21, 2026

A roundup of the 5 most significant IT news stories of the past few days.

## 1. usbliter8: an "unpatchable" BootROM exploit for iPhone XS–11, heir to checkm8

**What happened:** On June 18, researchers from Paradigm Shift published the details and a working PoC of the usbliter8 exploit — a SecureROM-level vulnerability in Apple A12 and A13 chips. The iPhone XS/XS Max/XR and the entire iPhone 11 lineup, a number of iPads, and the Apple Watch on S4/S5 chips are all in the line of fire. This is the first public BootROM exploit of this class since checkm8 (2019), extending the "immortal" jailbreak to the next generation of devices.

**Technical details:** The root of the problem is in the handling of USB setup packets by the Synopsys DesignWare controller in DFU mode: specially crafted packets corrupt memory and yield code execution directly in SecureROM. Because SecureROM is burned into silicon and cannot be updated after manufacturing, access survives firmware updates and device resets. Physical access and a USB connection are required.

**My take:** This is a classic example of the fact that a hardware root of trust is not magic but the same kind of code with bugs, only without the right to error: one slip in the USB stack at the manufacturing stage, and the device is compromised forever. The good news is that the vector requires physical access and DFU, so for most users this is not a "remote Armageddon" but rather a story about forensics, unlocked devices, and supply chains. The bad news is that A12/A13 are still in mass circulation as cheap and corporate devices, and for them there is now, in principle, no software fix. The main engineering lesson here is not about Apple but about the industry as a whole: a hard-burned bootrom with no ability to update is a trade-off between protection against modification and the impossibility of fixing anything, and we are once again seeing what it leads to.

## 2. AutoJack: a single web page turns an AI agent into RCE on the host

**What happened:** On June 18, the Microsoft Defender Security Research team disclosed AutoJack — an exploit chain in which an untrusted web page, opened by a browser-based AI agent (demonstrated on AutoGen Studio), reaches a privileged local service and launches arbitrary processes on the host. No credentials, login screens, or additional user actions are needed — it is enough to make the agent open a link (via a planted link, a URL field, or prompt injection).

**Technical details:** The chain glues together three weaknesses: an origin-allowlist bypass (the agent goes out as localhost and passes the checks), the absence of authentication on the MCP WebSocket endpoint, and the unsafe passing of parameters directly into the shell. Releases from PyPI are not affected, but two pre-release builds (0.4.3.dev1/.dev2) with the vulnerable handler remain unrevoked; the fix is already in main (commit b047730), but there is no stable patch yet.

**My take:** This, in my view, is the most important news of the period — not because of the specific bug in AutoGen, but because of the conclusion: localhost stopped being a trust boundary the moment a machine runs an agent that browses the untrusted web and simultaneously holds a privileged local control plane. For years we designed local services (MCP, dev servers, debug ports) on the assumption that "if it's localhost, it's one of ours," and AutoJack neatly shows that this assumption is dead. For everyone building agents: your MCP endpoint must have authentication and origin checks, and agent-generated code must live in a sandbox rather than call the shell with parameters "as received." I suspect AutoJack is the first warning bell of a whole class of vulnerabilities, and in the coming year we will see dozens of analogous CVEs across various agent frameworks.

**Novelty:** ★★★★★ | **Significance:** ★★★★☆ | **Impact:** ★★★★☆

## 3. FortiBleed: CISA records 86,644 compromised FortiGate devices

**What happened:** On June 19, CISA urged Fortinet customers to urgently protect their FortiGate devices against an ongoing campaign codenamed FortiBleed and attributed to Russian-speaking threat actors. As of June 19, 86,644 devices had been compromised. In parallel, CISA gave federal agencies just three days to remediate CVE-2026-20253 (unauthenticated remote code execution).

**Technical details:** FortiBleed is yet another attack on edge appliances: VPN gateways and firewalls sit at the perimeter, face the internet, and are updated slowly, which makes them an ideal entry point. A scale of tens of thousands of devices points to mass automated scanning and exploitation rather than targeted strikes.

**My take:** If AutoJack is the future, then FortiBleed is our unchanging present: the edge network box as the weakest link. The scenario repeats quarter after quarter (Ivanti, Citrix, Fortinet, Check Point) precisely because these devices are closed "black boxes" with their own firmware that administrators are afraid to update, lest they break connectivity. 86,000 devices is not about "genius hackers" but about a management failure: the absence of inventory, monitoring, and patching discipline at the perimeter. The practical takeaway is banal and therefore painful: edge devices must be patched first and monitored as critical infrastructure, not as "a box that just works."

## 4. RISC-V becomes the "third pillar": server chips enter the data center

**What happened:** Against the backdrop of Computex 2026 and the RISC-V Summit Europe (Bologna, June 8–12), RISC-V International head Andrea Gallo declared that the architecture has completed its transition from an academic project to an industrial standard. A wave of server processors has appeared: SiFive Performance P870D (up to 128 cores), Akeana Alpine, NextSilicon Arbel, and Epic Semi Contrail AIX (32 RISC-V cores + 16 AI cores, up to 75 TOPS). Analysts estimate RISC-V's share at roughly a quarter of the global market by core count.

**Technical details:** RISC-V's bet in the data center is an open ISA with no licensing royalties and the ability to add custom extensions tailored to specific workloads (move/process/secure data). For HPC and cloud, this means custom accelerators and CPUs honed for the task profile rather than a universal x86.

**My take:** For those who work with HPC and infrastructure, this is the most strategically important trend of the period. For years RISC-V lived in embedded systems and controllers while x86 and ARM split the server pie; now server-class processors with 128 cores are appearing — and that changes the equation. The open ISA is attractive precisely for its absence of vendor lock-in and the freedom of custom extensions, which is critical for HPC: you can bake in instructions for a specific domain. But I remain a pragmatist — the main barrier is not the silicon but the maturity of the software stack: compilers, optimized libraries (BLAS, MPI), drivers, support in distributions and build systems. Until the EasyBuild/Spack ecosystem and toolchains catch up to x86/ARM "out of the box," RISC-V in HPC production will be a niche for enthusiasts. The trend is right, but closing the gap will take another couple of years.

## 5. Vercel ships eve: "Next.js for agents" and Passport against shadow-AI

**What happened:** At the Ship conference in London (June 17), Vercel introduced eve — an open-source TypeScript framework for building and scaling agents, where "an agent is just a directory of files." Alongside it came Passport — an attempt to bring under corporate control the applications that employees build with AI (a fight against shadow-AI). CEO Guillermo Rauch reported that agents now produce more than half of commits at Vercel itself, up from less than 3% at the start of the year.

**Technical details:** eve is filesystem-first and "batteries included": durable execution (each step is checkpointed, a session survives a crash and a deploy, built on the open-source Workflow SDK), sandboxed compute (agent code is treated as untrusted and executed in a sandbox — Vercel Sandbox in production, Docker/microsandbox/just-bash locally), human-in-the-loop approvals, subagents, and evals out of the box.

**My take:** It is curious that eve ships on the very same day as AutoJack — and architecturally answers the same threat: agent code is untrusted by default and lives in a sandbox. This is the right shift from "let's quickly slap together an agent" to engineering discipline — durable execution, checkpoints, isolation, human-in-the-loop. I like the "agent as a directory of files" idea for its simplicity: it's versioned in git, human-readable, and not hidden behind framework magic. I'm skeptical of the "Next.js for agents" marketing — Vercel knows how to create standards, but the agent niche is already crowded (LangGraph, smolagents, Claude Agent SDK), and the winner is not the one who shipped a framework first but the one with a more mature ecosystem of adapters. Passport is more interesting than eve itself: the shadow-AI problem — apps that employees churn out with no regard for security — is real and underestimated, and it's good that it's starting to be addressed with tools rather than bans.
