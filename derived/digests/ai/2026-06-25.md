## 4. "AI will kill engineers" — but SignalFire's data says the opposite

**What happened:** SignalFire (June 24) published the State of Talent Report based on an analysis of the careers of millions of employees from 80M+ companies. The conclusion: engineering is the function most resistant to downturns. Overall hiring in big tech fell 25% versus the 2019 level, while engineering roles fell only 11%. The share of engineers among new hires at the 12 "Tech Majors" grew from 46% (2019) to 55% (2025); early-stage startups hired 7% more engineers than in 2019.

**My take:** This is a useful cold shower against the "AI Code Apocalypse" narrative. The data shows a different picture: AI hit designers and marketers harder, and the 2026 company is a "senior-heavy engineering core with the supporting structure stripped away around it." That is, AI doesn't remove engineers but removes the middle layer and raises the entry bar — which hits juniors and entry-level, not seniors. My takeaway for those in the profession or entering it: value is shifting from "writing code" to "designing, reviewing and being accountable for systems that AI partly writes." I'll caveat honestly: this is data from one VC fund for 2025, the "classified as Tech Majors" methodology is subjective, and I'd be wary of extrapolating to the whole market (especially outside the US). But as a signal against panic — it's valuable.

**What happened:** Hang Ten Systems, led by ex-Infosys CEO Vishal Sikka, came out of stealth with a $32M seed led by Mayfield (June 24). The idea — to help enterprises run and operate software more cheaply with the help of AI.

**My take:** This is an attempt to feel out a category still little talked about: not "AI writes code" but "AI operates and maintains already-written code" — monitoring, incidents, migrations, legacy support. If the coding agents of the past year solved the task of creation, here they aim at a much fatter and more boring pie — the TCO of operating software, where large enterprises have accumulated legacy for decades. Sikka's pedigree (SAP HANA, Infosys) gives access to the right CIOs, and that's half the battle in enterprise. My skepticism is elsewhere: "AI runs your software cheaper" is a promise easy to sell on a pitch deck and hard to hold in production, where the cost of an agent's error in operations is higher than in generation. $32M is a ticket to entry, not proof of the thesis. But I'd keep the AI-for-software-operations direction on the radar: it's closer to the industry's real pain than yet another copilot.
