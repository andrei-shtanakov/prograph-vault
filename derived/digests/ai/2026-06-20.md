## 2. Claude Mythos: Anthropic expands access to its "cyber-model" to ~150 organizations

**What happened:** Anthropic expanded the access program for Claude Mythos Preview — a model billed as a "watershed moment" for cybersecurity — to roughly 150 organizations under the Project Glasswing consortium. The model previously autonomously found thousands of zero-day vulnerabilities, including a hole in OpenBSD that had been hiding for 27 years.

**Architecture/Details:** Mythos is a general-purpose frontier model; the biggest gains over Opus 4.6 are in mathematics, long-context reasoning, software engineering and offensive security. It is deliberately NOT released to the general public — distribution runs only through trusted partners.

**My take:** This is the year's most interesting precedent for AI governance in practice rather than in words. Anthropic is effectively imposing "export control" on its own model — and that's a double-edged sword. On one hand, it's reasonable: a model that finds 27-year-old bugs in OpenBSD would, in open access, be a gift to any APT. On the other hand, expanding to 150 organizations dilutes the notion of "controlled access": the more partners, the higher the chance of weights or methodology leaking. I'm also uneasy about the asymmetry — defenders get the tool with a delay and under NDA, while attackers only need to reproduce the approach. For security engineers the takeaway is stark: assume automated zero-day discovery will soon become a commodity, and build that into your threat model now.

**My take:** For developers this is potentially more important than any new SoTA release. I'm watching execution closely: protocol wars are won not by the first release but by an ecosystem of adapters and real editor integrations. For those of us building tools, ACP is worth tracking now so we don't tie ourselves rigidly to a single CLI agent.
