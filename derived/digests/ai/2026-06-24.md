## 5. Dual-memory SNN: spiking networks get "slow memory" and stable training

**What happened:** Researchers from Imperial College London and ETH Zurich presented (June 2026) a dual-memory architecture of spiking neural networks (SNN) that combines fast spiking activity with an explicit slow low-dimensional memory channel. This stabilizes training and preserves event-driven sparsity — the main energy advantage of SNNs. Against the general trend, neuromorphic systems demonstrate up to 70× the speed and a thousandfold energy efficiency versus GPU-based edge AI on continual-learning tasks.

**Architecture/Details:** SNNs compute only on a "spike" — when a significant change occurs, not continuously — which gives sparsity and radical energy savings. The novelty is the added slow memory pathway, curing SNNs' chronic ailment: training instability over long horizons.

**My take:** While the mainstream fights over tokens per second on GPUs, neuromorphics is quietly working on a different axis — energy per inference, and this is an underrated direction. SNNs stalled for years precisely because they're hard and unstable to train; an explicit slow memory channel is a conceptually elegant move, echoing fast/slow weights and the same motif as in diffusion text: a move away from monoculture toward different trade-offs. The hard skepticism: this is a research result, not a product, and the history of SNNs is a graveyard of impressive lab metrics that didn't scale to real models and required exotic hardware. The "70×/thousandfold" figures are almost always about narrow edge cases, not frontier LLMs. But strategically, energy efficiency is exactly what will become the next bottleneck — you can't keep training and inferencing trillion-parameter models on current energetics forever. Neuromorphics is worth watching not for tomorrow's release but for what all this will run on in 5 years.
