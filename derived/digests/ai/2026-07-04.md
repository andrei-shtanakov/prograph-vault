## 1. Leanstral 1.5: Mistral made formal proofs cheap — $4 per task instead of $300

**What happened:** Mistral released Leanstral 1.5 — an open (Apache-2.0) agent model for theorem proving and code verification in Lean 4. The model "saturates" miniF2F and solves 587 of 672 PutnamBench problems, beating Seed-Prover 1.5 high by 7 problems — but at a cost of ~$4 per task versus ~$300 for the competitor. In a run over real code it found 5 previously unknown bugs across 57 repositories.

**Architecture/Details:** 119B MoE with 6.5B active parameters; a mid-training → SFT → RL pipeline with CISPO. Weights and a free API are already on Hugging Face, with a card in Mistral Docs.

**My take:** This is the most underrated story of the week, and it catches me more than any "flagship at +2% on MMLU." Formal verification remained for years a niche for academics and a couple of industrial teams — too expensive, too slow. A two-orders-of-magnitude drop in proof cost changes the economics: now you can seriously think about a CI pipeline where an agent doesn't just write code but formally proves its correctness in Lean. For me as an engineer this is closer to "vibe-coding with guarantees" than all the marketing about agents. The open license and 6.5B active parameters mean this can genuinely be run locally, not only through someone else's API. A cautious note: PutnamBench and miniF2F are math, and there's still a big distance to industrial Rust/C++ code, and "found 5 bugs" is a pretty but small experiment.

## 2. Microsoft merges Copilot into a single super-app and launches AutoPilot agents

**What happened:** According to The Decoder, in August Microsoft is merging its consumer and enterprise Copilot into a single application, cutting little-used features (like Copilot Podcasts) and adding a new class of agents — AutoPilot. This is a direct response to OpenAI and Anthropic's move toward an "AI super app."

**Architecture/Details:** AutoPilot is positioned as background autonomous agents over the unified Copilot runtime; there are no technical specs yet — the announcement is product-oriented, not research.

**My take:** What's interesting here isn't the technology but the strategic reversal. Microsoft spent a year and a half fragmenting Copilot into a dozen different products (Microsoft 365 Copilot, GitHub Copilot, Security Copilot, Copilot in Windows), and now the pendulum has swung back — toward consolidation. This is an honest admission that users got confused and that "one entry point" beats "ten specialized entry points." For developers the practical question is what happens to the API surface and whether the integration layer breaks in the merge. I'm skeptical of the word AutoPilot: the marketing escalation from "copilot" (assistant) to "autopilot" (autonomy) outpaces the real reliability of agents, and the cost of an error for an autonomous agent in corporate email is higher than for a suggestion. I'll wait for specs and granular permission controls before I believe it.

## 3. CVE explosion: 1,500 critical vulnerabilities in a month, but real risk barely rose

**What happened:** Epoch AI recorded a sharp jump: in June 2026, 21 organizations reported ~1,500 high-severity and critical CVEs — 3.5x higher than the previous monthly record. The spike coincided with the April release of Claude Mythos Preview, which can hunt vulnerabilities on its own. Anthropic's "Glasswing" program has already found 10,000+ serious holes; OpenAI has an analogous "Daybreak."

**Architecture/Details:** The annual forecast has been revised to ~66,000 CVEs (+46% over baseline). But the subset that really matters — with confirmed exploitation (CISA KEV) or high EPSS >10% — remains practically flat.

**My take:** This is the real shift that developers will feel before any new chatbot. We're entering an era where machines find bugs faster than people can triage and fix them. And here lies a trap: growth in the number of CVEs ≠ growth in real risk. If your team measures security by "number of open CVEs," you'll be swamped by noise, while the number of genuinely exploitable holes stays the same. The right response is not to panic over absolute figures but to rebuild triage around exploitability (KEV, EPSS) rather than severity. Separately alarming is the asymmetry: the same models that find holes for defenders also find them for attackers — and the defensive edge here isn't guaranteed. This is the story to show your security engineer.

**My take:** If the deal is confirmed, it's symbolic to the point of irony: Anthropic — Meta's direct competitor on frontier models — rents its "hardware."
