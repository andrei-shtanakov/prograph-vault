# AI Digest — June 26, 2026

A selection of the 5 most significant AI/ML news stories from the past few days.

## 1. OpenAI ships its own silicon: Jalapeño — the first custom chip for inference

**What happened:** On June 24 OpenAI and Broadcom unveiled Jalapeño — OpenAI's first proprietary "Intelligence Processor," an ASIC designed from scratch for LLM inference. The path from first design to tape-out took nine months — by the companies' claim, nearly the fastest development cycle for a high-performance ASIC in history. First deployments — by the end of 2026, gigawatt-scale with Microsoft and other partners.

**Architecture/Details:** A reticle-sized ASIC, a "clean sheet" for modern inference (not a reworking of a training accelerator). Claimed performance-per-watt substantially above current SOTA; part of the design optimization was done by OpenAI's own models. Industrialization is on Broadcom and Celestica (implementation, board, rack, networking).

**My take:** This is about vertical integration, not a "NVIDIA killer" — and that's exactly why it matters. OpenAI is repeating the trajectory of Google (TPU) and Amazon (Trainium): whoever pays tens of billions for compute sooner or later makes its own silicon to extract the middleman's margin and tune the hardware for its own serving stack. Nine months to tape-out is a figure to read skeptically (tape-out ≠ working data centers, with a year+ between them), but if true, then software-hardware co-design with models in the loop is a new pace. For us developers the direct effect is nil today and potentially large tomorrow: cheaper inference → cheaper API → more profitable agentic pipelines, which right now are cut precisely by token cost. I'm watching whether Jalapeño appears outside OpenAI at all or remains captive hardware for their products.

## 2. Vercel eve: "Next.js for agents" — an agent as a directory of files

**What happened:** At the Ship 26 conference in London (June 17), Vercel released eve — an open-source (Apache 2.0) agentic framework in TypeScript, where each agent = a directory of files compiled into a durable production service on Vercel Functions. CEO Guillermo Rauch stated that agents already trigger more than half the commits on the Vercel platform (versus <3% half a year ago). The current version is eve@0.11.4, public beta.

**Architecture/Details:** Six capabilities out of the box: durable execution, sandboxed compute, human-in-the-loop approvals, subagents, OpenTelemetry tracing and built-in evals. An agent is described by files: instructions and skills, model provider, tools, auth, channels, schedule. Start — `npx eve@latest init`.

**Architecture/Details:** Competition in the "provider-neutral" runtime niche sharply intensified — eve stands alongside LangGraph, CrewAI and Microsoft Agent Framework.

**My take:** The main idea here isn't technical but about packaging — "an agent is just a directory, the platform handles the rest." It's a strong DX move, and also a vendor lock-in hook: durable execution and sandboxes are seamless exactly as long as you're on Vercel Functions. Apache 2.0 formally frees your hands, but "compiles into a service on Vercel" hints at who pays for prod. Compared to LangGraph (state graph) and CrewAI (roles), eve bets on a file convention and durability-by-default — I find that closer for long-running tasks, where surviving restarts matters more than drawing complex graphs. It's too early to take into production on 0.11.4 (APIs are changing), but as a reference on architecture for durable agents it's a must-read now.

## 3. Cursor moves agents to the cloud, Claude Code turns a session into a page

**What happened:** Over June 17–18 a wave of developer-tool updates shipped. Cursor added cloud subagents (`/in-cloud`, `/babysit`) — you spin up subagents in isolated VMs, they prepare a PR remotely, and a cloud dev environment builds in under 10 minutes. Cursor 3.8 brought `/automate` (you describe automation in words — the agent itself wires up triggers and tools) plus a Slack-emoji trigger and five new GitHub triggers. Claude Code got Artifacts (beta): a session can be turned into an interactive page (a PR walkthrough or live dashboard) at a private link.

**Architecture/Details:** The common vector — moving agentic work off the local machine into cloud sandbox VMs and the shift from "chat with autocomplete" to declarative triggers (Slack, GitHub events) as entry points into an agent.

**My take:** This is a quiet but important shift: the coding agent stops being a "tab in the IDE" and becomes a background worker that lives in the cloud and reacts to events rather than to your cursor. `/babysit` is an honest name: at the current level of autonomy you still have to keep watch, and whoever says otherwise is selling something. What grabs me most is the trigger model: Slack emojis and GitHub events as an entry point — that's exactly the glue that was missing for agents to fit into a real team workflow without a separate orchestrator. The risk is clear — cloud subagents in someone else's VMs raise questions about secrets, repository access and audit; before letting `/in-cloud` loose on production code, I'd triple-check the token scope. But the direction is right: 2026 is the year agents move off the laptop into CI-like infrastructure.

## 4. Stellantis + Wayve + Uber: a global bet on L4 robotaxis

**What happened:** On June 17 Stellantis, Wayve and Uber signed a (non-binding) MoU on joint development and deployment of Level 4 robotaxis on a global scale. Stellantis provides "L4-Ready" platforms, Wayve — embodied-AI driving software, Uber — the mobility network and app. The agreement builds on already-existing ties: L2++ between Stellantis and Wayve and the Wayve–Uber partnership on launches in London, Tokyo and a dozen other cities.

**Architecture/Details:** Wayve's key technical thesis — an end-to-end embodied model that adapts to new regions without per-map city-by-city mapping (unlike Waymo's HD-map approach).

**My take:** An MoU is a press release, not a product, and "non-binding" here should be read literally: so far they've signed an intent, not a factory. But the balance of forces is interesting — this is an attempt to assemble an "anti-Waymo" alliance from hardware (Stellantis), brains (Wayve) and demand (Uber), where each covers what Waymo/Tesla lacks under one roof. Technically Wayve's bet on mapless end-to-end driving is the riskiest and most interesting piece: if scalability without HD maps really works, it radically changes the economics of deployment in new cities. For an AI engineer the lesson here isn't about cars but about the fact that embodied foundation models are coming out of labs into commercial alliances — and physical AI is monetized through partnerships, not through a single vertically integrated company. I hold my skepticism: L4 "globally" has been promised since 2017.

## 5. MiniMax M3: open weights, 1M context and native multimodality in one bottle

**What happened:** MiniMax released M3 — by its claim, the first open-weights model to combine frontier coding, a 1M-token context and native multimodality (text, image, video on input). Claimed 59.0% on SWE-Bench Pro (on par with GPT-5.5) and 83.5 on BrowseComp; the weights were published within ~10 days of the announcement. The price on OpenRouter — about $0.60/$2.40 per million input/output tokens (with a starting 50% discount).

**Architecture/Details:** Built on the proprietary MiniMax Sparse Attention (MSA) — it's precisely what pulls off the 1M context. An important caveat: the benchmarks are self-reported, run on MiniMax's own infrastructure.

**My take:** If the numbers are confirmed by independent runs, this is a serious bid, because "frontier coding + 1M context + multimodality + open weights" in one model has not come together until now. But precisely self-reported benchmarks on one's own hardware are a red flag I've learned not to ignore: paper results from Chinese open-weights labs regularly shrink on neutral harnesses. What's genuinely valuable regardless of leaderboards is open weights at a commercially sane price: this enables on-prem/private inference where data can't be sent to someone else's API, and for HPC scenarios and closed environments this is often the decisive factor, more important than an extra percent on SWE-Bench. MSA is another signal that sparse/linear attention mechanisms have become the de facto standard for long context rather than an exotic. I'd download it and run it on my own tasks before believing the table.
