## 3. GLM-5.2: the planet's best open-weights model under MIT and at 1/6 the price of GPT-5.5

**What happened:** Zhipu AI released GLM-5.2 (June 13) — an open-weights model with a genuine 1M context under the MIT license. Independent benchmarker Artificial Analysis ranks it #1 among open models and 4th overall, at roughly one-sixth the cost of GPT-5.5.

**Architecture/Details:** ~750B parameters in a sparse-MoE design, about 40B active per token, plus a new sparse-attention technique, IndexShare, to keep inference cost down on a 1M context. Weights released under MIT — with no regional or use-case restrictions.

**My take:** This is more important to me than any closed flagship: an MIT license on top-tier models means regulated industries and self-hosted teams get frontier class without vendor lock-in. IndexShare is the most interesting part technically: the 2026 race long ago shifted from "more parameters" to "how to hold a 1M context on budget," and every new sparse-attention scheme here is worth its weight in gold. My skepticism is on one point: Artificial Analysis benchmarks aren't your domain; "#1 among open models" doesn't mean "better for your task," especially on long context, where models love to lose the middle. But a 6x lower price with open weights — this is the case where you should just spin it up yourself and measure on your own data. I'd put GLM-5.2 as the first candidate to replace closed APIs where privacy and cost matter.

**Architecture/Details:** MAF is an open-source SDK and runtime with unified concepts and APIs for .NET and Python; hosted agents move orchestration into a managed service, and the harness provides a single lifecycle and Skills wiring.

**My take:** Against the backdrop of loud model launches, this is a quiet but, for us engineers, more important story: 2026 is the year agent frameworks stopped being a zoo of experiments and started consolidating into supported platforms. Merging AutoGen and Semantic Kernel into a single MAF is an admission that two competing SDKs from one company were a dead end. But I wouldn't rush to migrate: "hosted agents" in Foundry are convenience at the cost of new lock-in into Azure, and the harness/Skills concepts still need to be tested on real workflows, not on BUILD demos. The strong point is a unified API for .NET and Python — that's rare and genuinely lowers the barrier for enterprise teams on .NET. For me the takeaway is: watch and try in a sandbox, but keep agent architecture portable, without tying yourself to a proprietary runtime.
