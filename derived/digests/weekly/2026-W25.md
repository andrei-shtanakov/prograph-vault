## Key trends of the week

### 1. Governance overtook benchmarks: the state and the labs fight for control over access to models

The week's main axis is not "whose model is smarter" but "who decides who gets access to it." Frontier labs march in sync to the regulators, and for the first time the state applies an export-control-grade instrument to a commercial AI model. This is a qualitative shift: the lever of competition becomes the legal framework, not the Intelligence Index.

**Supporting events:**
- [06.20 AI] — Altman, Amodei, and Hassabis appeared together before G7 leaders for the first time (June 15–17) — three competitors marching in sync to the regulators.
- [06.20 AI] — Anthropic imposes a de facto "export control" on Mythos, expanding access only through trusted partners (Project Glasswing, ~150 organizations).
- [06.12–13, web] — The US government, via a national-security directive, forced Anthropic to **fully shut down** Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for everyone worldwide — the first time Washington has taken a commercial AI product offline.
- [06.17–18, web] — Amodei met personally with the Trump administration over export controls; no decision.

**Assessment:** Intensification of an existing trend, moved into a new phase — from lobbying to direct state intervention.
**Forecast:** In the next 2–4 weeks, expect either a partial return of Fable 5 (via citizenship-gating or geofencing) or a formalization of the export regime for specific capability classes (offensive security). For open source this is a risk: under the banner of safety, barriers against open weights can easily be smuggled in.

### 2. Standardizing access to models: model-agnostic layers and open agent protocols

In parallel with the governance battle, the industry is building abstraction layers to avoid vendor lock-in. Both platforms (Apple) and challengers (xAI/ACP) converge on a single idea — a unified interface to different models, an analog of what LSP became for editors and MCP for tools.

**Supporting events:**
- [06.20 AI] — In the iOS 27 beta, a Siri Extensions framework was found that routes requests to Claude/Gemini/ChatGPT with system-level provider selection.
- [06.20 IT] — Apple opens the Foundation Models framework (open source this summer) and admits third-party models into its Swift API — Claude and Gemini.
- [06.20 AI] — xAI Grok Build ships with support for the Agent Client Protocol (ACP) — an open agent↔client protocol portable between editors.

**Assessment:** A new trend gaining mass — a shift from vertical integration "one vendor — one client" to protocols.
**Forecast:** In the coming month, watch ACP vs MCP adoption and the actual code of Apple's open-source release. Protocol wars are decided by the ecosystem of adapters, not the first release — critical mass will be determined by the number of editor integrations by the end of summer.

The densest cross-thematic trend of the week. Classic perimeter holes meet AI that automates zero-day discovery — and it's precisely this pairing that provoked the state ban.

**Supporting events:**
- [06.20 IT] — CVE-2026-45657: wormable RCE in the Windows kernel (CVSS 9.8, TCP/IP stack, unauthenticated, SYSTEM privileges); 206 holes in the June Patch Tuesday.
- [06.20 IT] — A new extortion wave: Clop hits MOVEit again, ShinyHunters exploits a zero-day in Oracle PeopleSoft (CVE-2026-35273); victims — Kodak, Nintendo.
- [06.20 AI + 06.12 web] — Mythos autonomously found a 27-year-old zero-day in OpenBSD; it was precisely the **jailbreak of Fable 5**, which removed the guardrail against vulnerability discovery, that became the reason for the export ban.
- [06.20 IT] — Linux 6.18 LTS strengthens security hardening and reworks the slab allocator — a direct response to use-after-free exploits.

**Assessment:** Intensification of an existing trend with a new quality — automatic zero-day discovery turns from a research curiosity into a regulated threat.
**Forecast:** 2–4 weeks — high risk of a PoC/worm for CVE-2026-45657 (an EternalBlue scenario). Build into your threat model that offensive-security AI will soon become a commodity, and its distribution a subject of export control.

### 4. The battle for the data center: core density, long context, and capex under AI workloads

The infrastructure layer is adapting to the economics of inference. The bet shifts from peak gigahertz to density, perf/watt, and a cheap token — because it's precisely the cost of compute, not an extra two benchmark points, that determines who can serve frontier models.

**Supporting events:**

- [06.20 IT] — Linux 6.18 LTS as the base of the server fleet for years — the foundation under this infrastructure.

**Assessment:** Intensification of an existing trend; the novelty is Intel's admission that the frequency race in the data center is lost.
**Forecast:** Rising prices on GPUs and compute will heighten concentration: few will be able to serve a 2M context, which loops back to trend #1 (governance via infrastructure control).

## Top 5 events of the week

### 1. Wormable RCE in the Windows kernel (CVE-2026-45657)
**Date:** June 9 | **Category:** IT (Significance ★★★★★ + Impact ★★★★★ = 10)
The June Patch Tuesday closed 206 vulnerabilities, the flagship being an RCE in the Windows kernel's TCP/IP stack with CVSS 9.8: remote unauthenticated code with SYSTEM privileges. Current Windows 11 and Server are under threat.
**Why it matters:** "Wormable" over the network without authentication is a direct recipe for an EternalBlue/WannaCry-grade worm, especially for outward-facing RDP and IIS (plus CVE-2026-47291 in http.sys).
**For developers:** Patch immediately, not on the "next maintenance window"; perimeter audit for http.sys/RDP is priority #1.

### 2. Anthropic Mythos/Fable: from "export control" to a state ban
**Date:** June 12–20 | **Category:** AI (Significance ★★★★★ + Impact ★★★★ = 9)
Anthropic first expanded access to the "cyber-model" Mythos to ~150 organizations, but on June 12 the US government, via a national-security directive, forced the company to fully shut down Fable 5 and Mythos 5 — after someone jailbroke the guardrail against vulnerability discovery.

**For developers:** Access to top-tier models is now a function not only of price and rate limit but of geopolitics/citizenship. Don't build a critical pipeline on a single frontier model without a fallback.

### 5. Linux 6.18 becomes the new LTS kernel
**Date:** June | **Category:** IT (Significance ★★★★ + Impact ★★★★ = 8)

**Why it matters:** Large-scale exploits of recent years almost always came down to use-after-free and kernel memory management — each slab iteration reduces the attack surface.
**For developers:** If you run an HPC/server fleet — plan migration to 6.18 in advance, not at the moment your current kernel reaches EOL.

## Cross-analysis: AI ↔ IT

The week's main pairing is the Mythos/Fable story, which literally stitches AI and IT into a single plot. An AI model with offensive-security capabilities (Mythos found a 27-year-old bug in OpenBSD) meets the world of IT threats, where perimeter holes like a wormable RCE in the Windows kernel and ransomware via MOVEit/PeopleSoft are everyday reality. When someone jailbroke Fable 5, removing the guardrail against vulnerability discovery, abstract "AI governance" instantly became a concrete national-security IT incident — and the state responded with export control. This is a new class of risk: automatic zero-day discovery as a regulated dual-use technology.

The second pairing — hardware determines who even plays in big models. Gemini 3.5 Pro with a 2M context doesn't exist in a vacuum: behind it stand TPUs and the economics of a cheap token, while rising GPU prices (a shortage from the AI boom), the 288-core Xeon, and CloudBurst/Applied Digital's multi-gigawatt data centers are the infrastructure foundation that determines the cost of inference. The more expensive compute is, the narrower the circle of those who can serve frontier models — and this loops the infrastructure trend back to governance: control over data centers and chips becomes as much a lever as export directives.

The third, quieter pairing — Apple is building a bridge between OS infrastructure (IT) and models (AI): Siri Extensions and the open-source Foundation Models framework make a unified Swift API the entry point to Claude/Gemini/on-device. This is the same engineering idea as ACP and MCP — standardize the interface to models. For the industry, this means AI distribution is increasingly moving to the level of platforms and protocols rather than individual apps.
